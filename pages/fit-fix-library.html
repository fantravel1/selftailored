Every common garment fit problem — diagnosed, explained, and solved. Click any card for the full guide.

Jeans

Jeans Gap at the Waist

That annoying gap between your lower back and jeans waistband happens because standard jeans assume a fixed waist-to-hip ratio. Most women have a smaller waist relative to their hips than manufacturers account for.

Tailoring fix: Take in the back waistband ($15-25). A tailor removes fabric from the center-back seam and reshapes the waistband to follow your natural curve.
Shopping fix: Look for curvy-fit jeans (Levi's, Good American, NYDJ) that are designed with a higher waist-to-hip ratio difference. Try brands offering separate waist/hip sizing.
Quick fix: Waistband tightener clips ($5-8) or have a tailor add elastic to the back waistband ($10-15).
$15-25 Tailoring Common
Tops

Button-Down Pulls at the Bust

The dreaded button gape — the fabric pulls apart between buttons, creating a diamond-shaped opening that exposes your bra. This happens when the shirt fits your shoulders but is too small across the bust.

Tailoring fix: Add a hidden snap between buttons ($5-10) or move buttons to a new position ($5-10). For a permanent fix, size up and have the waist darted in ($15-25).
Shopping fix: Size up and tailor the waist. Or look for bust-friendly brands (Bravissimo, Pepperberry) that cut for a fuller bust. Wrap-style tops eliminate this problem entirely.
Quick fix: Double-sided fashion tape ($6-10) holds the gap closed for the day. Hollywood Fashion Secrets tape is a top pick.
$5-25 Tailoring Very Common
Blazers

Blazer Shoulders Too Wide

The shoulder seam hangs past your natural shoulder point, making the blazer look borrowed. This is one of the hardest — and most expensive — alterations. Shoulders are the skeleton of a garment.

Tailoring fix: Narrowing shoulders costs $75-150+ and is complex. Only worth it for high-quality blazers. A tailor must reconstruct the shoulder, armhole, and sometimes the sleeve.
Shopping fix: ALWAYS fit the shoulders first when buying blazers. Everything else can be altered more easily. Try petite sizes if your shoulders are narrow. Measure your actual shoulder width before shopping.
Styling fix: Structured shoulder pads can fill slightly wide shoulders. Roll the sleeves to create a more intentional oversized look.
$75-150+ Shopping Avoid Tailoring
Pants

Pants Bunch at the Ankle

Excess fabric pools around your ankles, making even expensive pants look sloppy. This is the single most common fit problem and the single easiest to fix. Every woman should know a good tailor for this alone.

Tailoring fix: A simple hem costs $10-15 and takes 1-3 days. Tell your tailor your preferred break: no break (clean at the shoe), half break (slight fold), or full break (classic fold). Bring the shoes you'll wear.
Shopping fix: Look for brands offering multiple inseam lengths (short, regular, long). Ankle-length and cropped styles avoid the problem entirely.
Quick fix: Iron-on hem tape ($5-8) creates a temporary hem without sewing. Works for an event or trial before committing to a permanent hem.
$10-15 Tailoring Most Common
Dresses

Dress Rides Up When Walking

Your dress creeps upward with every step, requiring constant pulling down. This happens due to static cling, fabric type (synthetic fabrics are the worst offenders), body friction, or the dress being too tight through the hips.

Tailoring fix: A tailor can add a weighted hem tape to the inside of the skirt ($10-15), which uses gravity to keep the dress in place. They can also add a lining if the dress lacks one ($25-40).
Shopping fix: Choose dresses with a lining. Natural fabrics (cotton, silk, wool) ride up less than synthetics. A-line silhouettes that skim the body have less friction than bodycon styles.
Quick fix: Anti-static spray, a slip underneath, or body powder on your thighs to reduce friction. Wearing bike shorts underneath solves the problem completely.
$10-40 DIY Common
Bras

Bra Straps Keep Showing

Visible bra straps peeking from necklines, off-shoulder tops, or wide-neck sweaters. This is a neckline-to-bra matching problem, not a bra problem — most women need 3-4 bra styles to cover all their necklines.

Tailoring fix: A tailor can add internal bra-strap holders (ribbon loops sewn inside the shoulder seam) to any top for $5-10. This keeps straps hidden and in place.
Shopping fix: Map your necklines to bra styles: racerback bras for tank tops, strapless for off-shoulder, plunge for V-necks, U-back for open-back tops. Convertible bras cover most situations.
Quick fix: Bra strap clips ($3-6) pull straps toward center back. Clear straps ($5-8) offer near-invisible support for occasional wear.
$3-10 Shopping Very Common
Skirts

Skirt Twists While Walking

Your pencil skirt or A-line rotates on your body so the zipper drifts to the front or side. This happens when the skirt fits loosely at the waist but snugly at the hips, creating a pivot point.

Tailoring fix: Take in the waist ($15-25) so it sits snugly. A tailor can also add a silicone grip strip to the inside waistband ($5-10) to prevent movement against your top.
Shopping fix: Look for skirts with a contoured waistband that follows your natural waist curve. Higher-waist styles with a closer fit through the waist are less likely to twist.
Quick fix: Tuck your top in to add friction. Wear a thin belt to cinch excess waist fabric. Safety pin the skirt to your tucked-in top as a last resort.
$15-25 Tailoring Common
Shoes

Shoes Slip at the Heel

Your heel lifts out of the shoe with each step, causing blisters and an awkward walk. This happens when the shoe's heel counter is too wide for your heel, or the shoe is slightly too long.

Cobbler fix: A cobbler can add a heel grip pad ($5-10) or add an extra insole to lift your foot and tighten the fit ($10-15). For leather shoes, they can shrink the heel area with heat and conditioning.
Shopping fix: Try different brands — heel shapes vary widely. Shoes with an ankle strap, slingback, or adjustable closure prevent heel slip. Try shoes on in the afternoon when feet are slightly swollen.
Quick fix: Adhesive heel grips ($5-8) add cushioning and reduce the space. Tongue pads push your foot back into the heel. Moleskin on the heel prevents blisters.
$5-15 DIY Very Common
Blazers

Blazer Sleeves Too Long

Sleeves that extend past your wrist make even an expensive blazer look ill-fitting and borrowed. The correct sleeve length should show about half an inch of shirt cuff underneath, or end at the wrist bone for a clean modern look.

Tailoring fix: Shortening blazer sleeves costs $15-25 for simple hems or $25-40 for functional buttonhole sleeves. This is one of the highest-impact alterations you can make.
Shopping fix: Try petite blazers if you are under 5'4". Measure your arm from shoulder point to wrist bone — this is your ideal sleeve length. Unlined blazers are easier and cheaper to alter.
Styling fix: Roll or push up blazer sleeves to a 3/4 length for a polished, modern look that bypasses the problem entirely. This is actually a fashion-forward styling choice.
$15-40 Tailoring High Impact
Dresses

Dress Too Tight at the Hips

The dress fits perfectly on top but pulls across the hips, creating horizontal wrinkles and restricting movement. This is a proportion mismatch — the bodice and skirt are cut for different body proportions than yours.

Tailoring fix: If there is seam allowance, a tailor can let out the side seams at the hip ($20-35). If the dress has a lining, both layers need adjustment. Adding a side slit can also add ease of movement ($15-25).
Shopping fix: Size for your largest measurement (usually hips) and have the waist and bodice taken in. Fit-and-flare or A-line dresses skim over hips instead of clinging. Stretchy fabrics forgive minor fit issues.
Style note: Horizontal wrinkles always point to where the garment is too tight. Vertical wrinkles point to where it is too loose. Learn to read these wrinkle signals.
$20-35 Tailoring Common
Tops

Shoulder Seam Falls Off the Shoulder

The shoulder seam droops past your natural shoulder point, creating a sloppy, oversized look. This is usually because the garment is cut for wider shoulders than yours, or you have a narrower frame than standard sizing assumes.

Tailoring fix: A tailor can move the shoulder seam inward ($25-45), but this is a moderate alteration that also requires adjusting the sleeve. Worth it for quality pieces, not for fast fashion.
Shopping fix: Try petite sizes or brands that cater to smaller frames (Ann Taylor Petite, J.Crew). Raglan sleeves and set-in sleeves fit different shoulder widths — know which works for you.
Quick fix: Choose tops with gathering, ruching, or drop-shoulder styling that intentionally sits off the shoulder. What looks "sloppy" in a structured top looks "cool" in a relaxed one.
$25-45 Shopping Common
Jeans

Jeans Wear Out at Inner Thighs

The inner thigh area of your jeans develops thin spots, holes, and fraying within months. This is caused by friction between your thighs when walking — and it happens to most women, regardless of body size. It is not a reflection of your body.

Tailoring fix: A tailor can reinforce the inner thigh area with iron-on patches on the inside ($10-15) before the damage starts. Once holes appear, darning or patching costs $15-25.
Shopping fix: Choose jeans with a higher cotton content and heavier denim weight (12oz+). Wider leg styles reduce friction. Reinforced crotch gussets (found in workwear brands) extend lifespan dramatically.
Prevention: Wash jeans less frequently (every 5-10 wears) to preserve fiber integrity. Apply beeswax or denim wax to the inner thigh area to reduce friction. Rotate between multiple pairs.
$10-25 DIY Very Common
Bras

Bra Band Rides Up in Back

The back of your bra band creeps upward throughout the day, angling higher than the front. This is the #1 sign your band is too large. The band provides 80% of support — not the straps. A rising band means the band is not gripping your ribcage firmly enough.

Fit fix: Go down a band size and up a cup size to maintain the same volume (e.g., 36C to 34D). The band should be snug on the loosest hook when new, giving you room to tighten as it stretches over time.
Shopping fix: Get professionally fitted. 80% of women wear the wrong bra size. Use the scoop-and-swoop method when trying bras: lean forward, put on the bra, then scoop all breast tissue into the cups from under your arms.
Quick fix: A bra band extender used temporarily on a too-tight new bra. Tighten the straps slightly, but remember: straps should be adjusted last, not first.
$0 Shopping Very Common
Shoes

Shoes Too Narrow or Pinching

Your shoes squeeze at the ball of the foot, pinch your toes, or create painful pressure points. Standard width shoes are designed for a B width — but many women need a C or D (wide) width, especially as feet naturally widen with age.

Cobbler fix: A cobbler can stretch leather shoes up to half a size wider ($15-25) using professional stretching tools. Suede stretches more easily than patent leather. Synthetic shoes generally cannot be stretched.
Shopping fix: Brands offering wide widths: Naturalizer, Cole Haan, New Balance. Measure both feet — most people have one foot larger. Always buy for the larger foot. Shop in the afternoon when feet are naturally more swollen.
DIY fix: Shoe stretching spray ($8-12) + a shoe stretcher ($15-20) used overnight can stretch leather shoes at home. Wearing thick socks with a hairdryer on leather also works for minor stretching.
$8-25 DIY Common
Pants

Pants Pulling or Bunching at Crotch

Fabric gathers at the crotch, creating "smile lines" (curved wrinkles) or a visible camel toe. This is a rise issue — the crotch seam is either too short (pulling) or too long (bunching) for your body proportions.

Tailoring fix: A tailor can adjust the rise by letting out or taking in the crotch curve ($20-30). They can also add a crotch gusset for more room if needed.
Shopping fix: If crotch bunches: you need a shorter rise (try mid-rise). If crotch pulls: you need a longer rise (try high-rise). Measure your rise from waistband to crotch seam along the front to compare brands.
Style note: "Smile lines" radiating from the crotch always indicate the rise is too short. Excess fabric pooling at the crotch indicates the rise is too long. Once you learn to read these signs, you can self-diagnose instantly.
$20-30 Tailoring Common
Tops

Top Rides Up Over the Belly

Your top keeps inching upward and bunching above your waist, especially when sitting or bending. This happens when the top is too short, too fitted through the torso, or when the fabric lacks the weight to drape downward over curves.

Tailoring fix: If the hem has a generous turn-up, a tailor can let it down for extra length ($10-15). Adding a hidden elastic or gripper at the hem helps it stay in place ($10-15).
Shopping fix: Look for tunic-length or longer torso tops. Choose fabrics with weight and drape (rayon, modal, tencel) over stiff lightweight fabrics. Front-tucking into high-waisted bottoms solves this elegantly.
Quick fix: A shirt-stay elastic loop (hooks from shirt hem to sock or thigh band) keeps the shirt tucked for military-level neatness. Hollywood Fashion Tape at the hem also works for short sessions.
$10-15 Shopping Common

Fit Fix FAQ

Everything you need to know about diagnosing and fixing garment fit problems.

A garment is worth tailoring if it meets these criteria: 1) The fabric quality is decent — no pilling, thinning, or poor construction. 2) It fits well in the hardest-to-alter areas (shoulders, armholes, crotch curve). 3) The needed alteration is simple (hem, waist, darts, sleeves). 4) The tailoring cost is less than 50% of the garment's replacement cost. A $30 blouse with $15 in darts that fits like a $200 piece is always worth it. A $15 fast-fashion top that needs $40 in alterations is not.

Bring the exact undergarments and shoes you will wear with the garment. For pants, wear the shoes you intend to pair them with — heel height changes hemline length significantly. Bring reference photos showing how you want the finished garment to look. Know your preferred pant break (no break, half break, full break), your desired sleeve length, and how you want the garment to feel (snug, comfortable, relaxed). A good tailor will pin everything in place so you can see the result before they cut.

Avoid tailoring these: Shoulders too narrow — you cannot add structure where there is none. Garments more than 2 sizes too large — the proportions break down completely. Heavily patterned items (plaids, stripes, large prints) where altering seams would misalign the pattern. Leather and suede — needle holes are permanent. Heavily beaded or embellished pieces — the work is too delicate and time-consuming. Lined garments where you want to resize by more than one size — both layers need adjusting. The golden rule: fit the shoulders first when shopping, tailor everything else.

Here is a realistic cost guide for common alterations: Hem pants: $10-15. Hem dress: $15-25. Take in waistband: $15-25. Add darts to a blouse: $15-30. Shorten sleeves (simple): $15-20. Shorten blazer sleeves (with buttons): $25-40. Taper pant legs: $15-25. Move buttons: $5-10. Add hidden snaps: $5-10 per snap. Take in side seams: $20-35. Prices vary by location and tailor skill — expect to pay 20-40% more in major cities. Always get a quote before committing.

Wrinkles are your garment's way of telling you what is wrong: Horizontal wrinkles point to where the garment is too tight — the fabric is being stretched horizontally and fighting back. Vertical wrinkles indicate excess fabric — the garment is too loose or too long in that area. Diagonal wrinkles radiating from a point show where the garment is being pulled by tension (like "smile lines" from a too-short crotch). Pulling at specific points (bust, hips, shoulders) means that area needs more room while other areas may need less. Learn to read these signals and you can self-diagnose any fit problem in seconds.

The golden rule: always size for your largest measurement and then have the rest taken in. It is far easier to remove fabric than to add it. If your shoulders are a size 8 but your hips are a size 12, buy the 12 and have the shoulders and waist adjusted. If your bust is larger relative to your frame, size up for the bust and dart the waist. The exception: heavily structured garments (blazers, coats) should be sized for the shoulders, since shoulder alterations are the most expensive and complex. Bottom line: taking in is cheap and easy. Letting out is limited and sometimes impossible.

Start by asking friends, coworkers, or local boutique shops for recommendations — boutiques often have a go-to tailor they trust. Check Google and Yelp reviews, focusing on reviews that mention specific alterations similar to what you need. Test a new tailor with a simple alteration first (like hemming pants) before trusting them with complex or expensive pieces. A good tailor will: pin everything before cutting, ask what shoes you'll wear, explain limitations honestly, and suggest alternatives if an alteration is not advisable. Avoid tailors who refuse to pin or who seem rushed during fittings.

Many fit problems have DIY solutions: Fashion tape fixes button gape, neckline droop, and strap slipping ($6-10 per roll). Iron-on hem tape creates a no-sew temporary hem ($5-8). Waistband clips tighten jeans that gap at the waist ($5-8). Heel grips and insoles fix shoe slipping ($5-10). Anti-static spray stops dresses from clinging or riding up ($5-7). Bra strap clips convert regular straps to racerback ($3-6). Shoe stretching spray + stretcher widen tight shoes ($15-25). These quick fixes are perfect for occasional wear or testing whether you like a fit before committing to permanent alterations.
