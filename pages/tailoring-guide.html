The Complete Guide

Tailoring 101

Everything you need to know about alterations — costs, glossary, checklists, and the smartest tailoring decisions you can make. Tailoring is the most underused cheat code in women's fashion.

Know Before You Go

Tailoring Cost Guide

Most alterations cost less than a new garment. Here is what you can expect to pay for the 12 most common women's alterations. Prices are typical U.S. averages and may vary by location and complexity.

$10 — $15

Hem Pants

The single most impactful alteration. Clean hems transform any pair of pants from sloppy to polished. Works on jeans, trousers, and wide-legs. Ask for a blind hem for an invisible finish on dress pants.

$15 — $25

Taper the Leg

Slim the leg from knee to ankle for a modern, streamlined silhouette. Turns frumpy bootcuts into sleek straight-legs. One of the best ways to update older pants without buying new ones.

$15 — $20

Shorten Sleeves

Too-long sleeves make even expensive blazers look borrowed. This quick fix has massive visual impact. For blazers, the sleeve should end at the wrist bone, showing a sliver of shirt cuff underneath.

$15 — $30

Add Darts

Darts create shape where there is none. Transform a boxy blouse into a fitted masterpiece that follows your curves. Back darts, bust darts, or waist darts — each targets a different fit problem.

$15 — $25

Take In the Waist

The waistband fix for jeans, skirts, and trousers. If your waist-to-hip ratio causes a gap at the back of every pair of pants, this one alteration solves it. One of the most requested alterations worldwide.

$5 — $10

Move Buttons

A tiny shift in button placement can eliminate gaping and improve the entire fit of a garment. This is the cheapest and fastest alteration — and it solves the dreaded bust-gap on button-downs instantly.

$15 — $25

Shorten a Dress

The wrong hemline can ruin an otherwise perfect dress. Whether you are going from midi to knee-length or adjusting a maxi, hemming a dress transforms its proportions and makes it feel intentional.

$25 — $40

Add Lining

Lining makes cheap fabrics drape better, prevents static cling, and adds a layer of comfort. It turns a see-through blouse into a wearable piece and makes inexpensive skirts hang like designer ones.

$20 — $35

Let Out Seams

If a garment is slightly too tight, a tailor can let out the seams for extra room. This only works if there is enough seam allowance — usually 0.5 to 1 inch is available. Check the inside seams before committing.

$15 — $25

Replace Zipper

A broken zipper does not mean the garment is dead. Replacing a zipper is one of the most common repairs and gives a second life to dresses, skirts, and pants. Invisible zippers cost slightly more.

$40 — $60

Adjust Shoulders

The most advanced alteration on this list. Shoulder adjustments involve restructuring the entire top of a garment. Only worthwhile on investment pieces like blazers, coats, or suits. Find a specialist tailor.

$20 — $30

Hem a Jacket

A jacket that is too long throws off your entire silhouette. Cropping it to just the right length — at the hip bone for most women — creates the illusion of longer legs and a cleaner overall line.

The Smart Decision

What to Tailor vs. What Not to Tailor

Not every garment is worth the trip to the tailor. Here is how to decide what is worth altering and what to skip.

DO Tailor These

  • Pants that are too long. Hemming is cheap, fast, and the most impactful alteration you can get. Never let pants bunch at the ankle.
  • Button-downs that gap at the bust. Moving buttons or adding a hidden snap costs under $10 and solves this instantly.
  • Dresses that are almost perfect but slightly too long. Hemming a dress changes its entire proportion and can make a $40 dress look like $200.
  • Blazers with too-long sleeves. Sleeve length is one of the biggest signals of fit quality. Get them shortened to the wrist bone.
  • Jeans that gap at the waist. Taking in the waistband eliminates the most common fit complaint in women's denim.
  • Boxy blouses. Adding darts takes 10 minutes and creates shape that transforms the garment from shapeless to structured.
  • Vintage or thrift finds that are close but not quite right. Tailoring is what makes secondhand shopping actually work.
  • Investment pieces you plan to wear 50+ times. Spending $30 to perfect a $150 blazer is one of the best style ROI decisions you can make.

DON'T Tailor These

  • Garments that are more than 2 sizes too big. Beyond two sizes, the entire garment structure falls apart — armholes, seam lines, and proportions shift too much.
  • Cheap fast-fashion made from ultra-thin fabric. The material will not hold up to alteration. The seams will pucker and the fabric may tear at the needle.
  • Heavily beaded or embellished pieces. Beading makes alterations extremely time-consuming and expensive. The cost usually exceeds the garment's value.
  • Leather, suede, or PU leather at a standard tailor. These materials require specialized tools and expertise. A regular tailor can ruin leather permanently.
  • Garments with complex prints that need matching. Plaid, stripes, and large-scale patterns must be aligned at every seam. This adds significant cost and skill requirements.
  • Anything where the alteration cost exceeds 50% of the garment value. The math does not work. Spend that money on a garment that fits better from the start.
  • Shoulder restructuring on inexpensive jackets. At $40-60, shoulder work costs more than most fast-fashion blazers. Save it for investment-grade outerwear.
  • Trendy items you will only wear one season. Tailoring is an investment in longevity. Do not spend tailoring money on pieces with a short lifespan.
Be Prepared

What to Bring to Your Tailor

A successful tailor appointment starts before you walk in the door. Bring these items to get the best possible result from every alteration.

👟

The Shoes You Will Wear

Heel height changes everything. Pants hemmed with flats will be too short with heels, and vice versa. Always bring the exact shoes you plan to pair with the garment. If you wear it with multiple shoes, hem to the tallest pair and accept a slight break with flats.

👙

The Right Undergarments

Wear the bra, shapewear, or slip you plan to wear under the garment. Different bras create different bust shapes, and shapewear changes your silhouette. The tailor needs to see the real picture.

📷

Reference Photos

A picture is worth a thousand words — especially for hemline length, taper angles, and neckline adjustments. Show your tailor exactly what you want. Save screenshots from Instagram, Pinterest, or this site.

👜

Belts and Accessories

If you wear the garment with a belt, bring the belt. It changes where the waist falls and how much fabric needs adjustment. Same for scarves that change necklines or bags that hit at the hip.

📝

A Clear Description of the Problem

Be specific. Instead of "it doesn't fit right," say "the waist gaps about two inches in the back when I sit down" or "the sleeves cover my knuckles." Precision helps your tailor deliver exactly what you need.

💰

A Budget in Mind

Know your limit before the appointment. A good tailor will tell you what is possible within your budget and prioritize the most impactful alterations first. Never feel pressured into spending more than you planned.

Speak the Language

Tailoring Glossary

Know the terms before you visit your tailor. Understanding the vocabulary helps you communicate exactly what you want — and understand what they recommend.

A stitched fold of fabric that shapes a flat piece of cloth to fit the body's curves. Darts are typically found at the bust, waist, or back of a garment. Adding darts to a boxy top transforms it from shapeless to sculpted. There are straight darts, curved darts, and French darts — each creates a slightly different silhouette.

The extra fabric between the stitch line and the raw edge of the fabric. Seam allowance determines how much a garment can be let out. Standard seam allowance is 5/8 inch, but some garments — especially fast fashion — have very little. Check the inside seams before asking a tailor to let something out; if the allowance is minimal, letting out is not possible.

A hemming technique where the stitches are invisible from the outside of the garment. The needle catches only a tiny thread of the outer fabric, making the hem look like it was never altered. Ideal for dress pants, skirts, and formal dresses. Ask for a blind hem when you want a clean, professional finish with no visible stitch line.

Gradually narrowing a garment — usually the leg of pants — from one point to another. Tapering from the knee to the ankle turns a wide-leg or bootcut pant into a straight or slim-leg. The key is specifying where the taper should start and how narrow you want the opening. Bring reference photos for best results.

Reducing the width of a garment by sewing the seams closer together. Taking in the waist means making the waistband smaller. Taking in the sides means making the overall garment narrower. The opposite of letting out. This is one of the most common alterations and works on almost every garment type.

The opposite of taking in — releasing existing seams to make a garment slightly larger. This only works if there is sufficient seam allowance (extra fabric inside the seams). Higher-quality garments typically have more seam allowance, making them easier to let out. You can usually gain 0.5 to 1 inch per seam.

A long, curved seam running vertically from the shoulder or armhole over the bust and down to the hem. Princess seams replace darts by building shape directly into the seam lines. Garments with princess seams are easier to alter for bust fit because the tailor can adjust the curve of the seam rather than adding separate darts.

The difference between your body measurements and the garment measurements. Ease is the extra room built into a garment for comfort and movement. Zero ease means the garment fits like a second skin. Negative ease means the fabric stretches. Understanding ease helps you communicate whether you want a garment fitted, comfortable, or relaxed.

Fabric cut diagonally across the grain, at a 45-degree angle. Bias-cut garments drape beautifully and cling to the body's curves. However, they are harder and more expensive to alter because the fabric stretches and moves unpredictably. If you need a bias-cut garment altered, find a tailor who specializes in this technique.

A layer of stiff or semi-stiff fabric fused or sewn to the inside of a garment to add structure and support. Interfacing is found in collars, cuffs, waistbands, and button plackets. It is what makes a shirt collar stand up and a waistband hold its shape. When a tailor works on these areas, they must account for the interfacing layer.

The fold of fabric that forms where pants meet the shoe. A "full break" means a deep fold with fabric pooling. A "half break" has a slight fold. "No break" means the pants just touch the top of the shoe with zero folding. For women's pants, a clean half-break or no-break usually looks the most polished and modern.

The small supplies used in garment construction and alteration: buttons, zippers, snaps, hooks, thread, elastic, and more. A good tailor stocks standard notions, but if your garment has unusual buttons or a specialty zipper, you may need to source replacements yourself. Bring extras if you have them.
